## The idea
Inspired by a TikTok showing 3 friends arguing (in a funny, not mean-spirited way) over who liked a particular video, spotted in a shared/mutual "Liked Videos" tab on TikTok. This project is an original interpretation of that as a small web app / party game for a friend group.

## The game concept
1. Friends maintain a shared TikTok "burner" account that they all follow / are mutual friends with, so each friend's individual "Liked Videos" tab is visible to the others within the TikTok app (assuming that visibility setting is turned on).
2. **Important technical constraint:** TikTok has no public API for reading a user's Liked Videos list — not even for a mutual-friend "burner" account. The only way to see it is by manually opening TikTok and looking. Automating this (scraping a logged-in session) would violate TikTok's Terms of Service, risk getting the account banned, and is technically fragile (private/undocumented endpoints, anti-bot protections) — so this was deliberately ruled out as a build approach.
3. Instead, the workflow is: **a human manually checks a friend's Liked tab on real TikTok** (this can be done in a batch, ahead of time — no need to do it "live"), copies a video link, and **manually enters it into our app**, tagging which friend it belongs to. This entry is kept **hidden** from the group.
4. The app then automates everything else:
   - Randomly draws one un-drawn video from the pool for a "round," without revealing whose it is.
   - Lets the other friends **vote/guess** which friend liked it.
   - **Reveals** the true answer once voting is done.
   - Updates a **leaderboard** (points for correct guesses).
5. Videos don't need to be embarrassing/controversial — literally any liked video works. The fun is the social guessing/reveal moment, not the content itself.

## Roles (revised: lobby-based + self-submission, decided 2026-07-07)
The game is no longer a single fixed friend-group — it now uses **lobbies**, so it can scale to multiple independent friend groups without hardcoding anyone. Video submission is also now **self-submission** (see "Video collection" section below) rather than one person tagging someone else's video.
- **Guest** — has an account but isn't in any lobby yet.
- **Player** — logged in, submits videos to their own personal pool (paste a link to a video *they themselves* liked — see below), joins a lobby via a **6-digit join code**, or creates a new lobby (becoming its Organizer). Inside a lobby's game: votes each round, sees the reveal once everyone's guessed, views live/final standings, edits their own profile, and **leaves a lobby** (new, 2026-07-07). Scoped to whichever lobby(s) they've joined — not global.
- **Organizer** — **per-lobby, not global.** Starts as whoever *created* the lobby, but the role can **transfer**: if the Organizer leaves the lobby and other members remain, the role automatically passes to whoever **joined earliest** among those remaining (revised 2026-07-07 — originally decided "fixed to creator, no transfer," but that didn't hold up once lobbies became temporary/leavable). Superset of Player within that lobby: **start a game** (kicks off an 8-round game — see "Game session mechanics" below; this replaced the earlier "trigger a new draw" / "reset standings" actions once games became round-based), invite players (share the join code), and (new, 2026-07-07) **kick a player from the waiting room** — see "Kicking players" under Game session mechanics. Does **not** manage the evidence pool — that's Admin-only (see below). The same person can be Organizer of one lobby and just a Player in someone else's.
- **Admin** — **global**, above all lobbies. Decided to be **just the trainee's own account** (a single `is_admin` flag), not a general admin-management feature. Can: see a list of all open lobbies, manage the player roster (view/remove registered accounts across all lobbies), and delete any video/post across any lobby (moderation/cleanup).

## Lobby lifecycle: lobbies are temporary (decided 2026-07-07)
Lobbies are **not permanent** — a lobby only exists while it has at least one member in it.
- **Leaving:** Players (and the Organizer) can leave a lobby. This is a new Player action.
- **Organizer leaves, others remain:** the Organizer role **transfers automatically** to whoever **joined that lobby earliest** among the remaining members (see the revised Organizer definition above).
- **Last member leaves:** the lobby **closes** (`is_open = false`). At that point:
  - **Guesses/points/standings for that lobby are wiped.** They're explicitly scoped to a live lobby and don't persist once it's gone — re-affirmed 2026-07-07 that draws and standings are always exclusive to one lobby, never shared or carried over.
  - **Videos (`posts`) are preserved**, not deleted, even though the lobby that held them is now closed. Nothing a player submitted is ever lost.
- This means closing a lobby is a **soft close** (flip a flag, cascade-delete its games/rounds/guesses), not a hard delete of the `lobbies` row.

## Video collection: how videos get into the app (revised 2026-07-07)
We explored and **rejected** a "burner TikTok account" idea (friends befriend a shared burner account and send it their liked videos, with Admin reading its inbox) — walked through why below, since it's a good lesson in a recurring constraint:
- **Why it doesn't work as automation:** TikTok has no public API for reading an account's inbox/DMs, same as it has none for reading a Liked-videos tab. "Automatically" detecting videos sent to a burner account would require logging into that account and scraping its messages behind the scenes — the same unauthorized-automation problem as the original Liked-tab-scraping idea we ruled out at the very start (ToS violation, ban risk, fragile private endpoints). Routing through a burner account doesn't remove that constraint; it just moves *what* would need scraping.
- **Final decision: self-submission.** Drop the burner account entirely. Each player pastes a link to a video **they personally liked** directly into the app's own "File Evidence" form. This is fully automatable (a normal form submission to our own Supabase database, not scraping TikTok), has zero ToS risk (nothing touches TikTok's systems), and needs no burner account to create or maintain. The owner of a video and its submitter are now always the same person — simplifies the schema (see below).
- **Future enhancement (not MVP): Web Share Target.** If the app is installed as a PWA (added to home screen), it's technically possible to register it in the phone's native "Share" sheet, so a player can tap Share on a TikTok video and land directly in a pre-filled Add Evidence screen (just press Add). **Important limitation: Android/Chrome only — iOS/Safari does not support the Web Share Target API for web apps**, so iPhone users would always need the manual copy-paste fallback regardless. Also adds real setup complexity (PWA manifest, HTTPS hosting) beyond the plain-webpage MVP. Decision: **build the simple paste-a-link MVP first, revisit the share-target enhancement later** once the core game works end-to-end.

## Chosen tech stack (and why)
- **Frontend:** plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No framework chosen. Chosen partly because it's simple to learn and doesn't require a build toolchain.
- **Backend + Auth + Database: Supabase.** Chosen specifically because:
  - It's free with **no credit card required**, and the free tier has no time limit (though free *projects* pause after ~7 days of inactivity — resumable manually).
  - It's positioned as an **open-source alternative to Firebase** (which the trainee explicitly wanted to avoid investing money into).
  - It bundles a login/auth system *and* a Postgres database, both callable **directly from frontend JavaScript** via the `supabase-js` client library — meaning **no custom backend server (e.g. Node.js or Java) is required** for this project. This was a deliberate simplification given the trainee's experience level.
- **Hosting:** free static hosting (Netlify, Vercel, or GitHub Pages — any works, none chosen definitively yet). Decision was made to **host online from the very start**, not build locally first, because each friend needs to access it from their own device with their own login.
- **Multi-user, not shared-device:** each friend gets their own account/login. This was an explicit choice over a simpler single-shared-device version, because the trainee wanted real per-person accounts from the start.

## Game session mechanics (decided 2026-07-07)
Reorganizing the app's navigation (see mockup v3 below) surfaced a fuller picture of how a "game" actually runs, which changes the schema:
- **Video submission is a personal pool, not lobby-scoped.** You submit videos from a central "Home" area, independent of any one lobby. Any lobby you're a member of can draw from your submitted videos when it runs a game — a video isn't "used up" by one lobby either; the same video could in principle be drawn in different lobbies' games over time.
- **A lobby has a waiting room.** After joining/creating a lobby, players land in a waiting room (member list + lobby code) until the Organizer clicks **Start Game**.
- **A game is a fixed sequence of ~8 rounds.** Each round draws one not-yet-used-in-this-game video from the current members' combined personal pools, players vote, and — revised from the earlier "instant reveal on your own vote" — the app now **waits until everyone in the lobby has guessed**, then reveals simultaneously and updates standings for the whole group at once, before auto-advancing to the next round.
- **After round 8, the game finalizes** into a final-results screen.
- **The same lobby can play again.** The Organizer can start a fresh game any time (as long as the lobby still has members); **standings reset to zero for each new game** — they never carry over from a finished game to the next one.
- **Lobby closing is unaffected by this:** the lobby itself still only closes when its last member leaves (see "Lobby lifecycle" above), independent of whether a game is mid-session or finished.
- **Kicking players (decided 2026-07-07):** the Organizer can remove a player from their lobby, but **only from the waiting room** — once a game is live, membership is locked until it finishes (no kicking mid-round). A kicked player is **not banned**: they can rejoin later with the lobby's code like anyone else. Mechanically this is identical to that player leaving on their own — no new schema needed, it's just deleting their `lobby_members` row (if they were Organizer at the time, the usual transfer-to-earliest-remaining-member rule applies, same as a normal leave).
- **No joining mid-game (decided 2026-07-07):** once the Organizer clicks Start Game, that lobby's join code stops working for new members — joining is only allowed while the lobby has no active (started-but-not-finalized) game. It opens back up automatically once the game finalizes. Backend rule: reject a new `lobby_members` insert if that lobby has a `games` row with `started_at` set and `finalized_at` still null.

## Planned database schema (revised 2026-07-07 for game sessions)
Seven tables in Supabase/Postgres:
- **`profiles`** — one row per person: `id` (linked to Supabase Auth user), `display_name`, `is_admin` (bool, default false — true only for the trainee's own account). (No TikTok-handle field — considered, then dropped since self-submission means there's nothing left to match it against.)
- **`lobbies`** — one row per game lobby: `id`, `code` (unique 6-digit join code), `name` (optional), `organizer_id` (→ profiles.id — the **current** Organizer; mutable, since the role can transfer), `created_by` (→ profiles.id — who originally created it; immutable, kept only for history/display), `created_at`, `is_open` (flips to false when the lobby empties out — a soft close, not a row delete).
- **`lobby_members`** — join table: `lobby_id`, `user_id`, `joined_at` — tracks which players are in which lobby. `joined_at` is also what determines Organizer-transfer order (earliest remaining joiner wins) and lets the app detect "last member just left."
- **`posts`** — one row per submitted video: `submitted_by` (→ profiles.id — also the owner/liker, since submission is self-submission), video URL, timestamp. **No `lobby_id` anymore** — it's a personal pool (revised 2026-07-07), not tied to any one lobby. **Never deleted.**
- **`games`** (new, 2026-07-07) — one row per game session played in a lobby: `id`, `lobby_id`, `started_at`, `finalized_at` (null while in progress), `round_count` (default 8). A lobby can have many games over its lifetime, one at a time.
- **`game_rounds`** (new, 2026-07-07) — one row per round within a game: `id`, `game_id` (→ games.id), `round_number`, `post_id` (→ posts.id — the video drawn for this round, hidden until reveal), `revealed_at`. Replaces the old "drawn" flag on `posts`, since a post's drawn/undrawn status is now specific to one game, not global.
- **`guesses`** — one row per vote: `round_id` (→ game_rounds.id — not directly to a post anymore, since a post can appear in different games), `guesser_id`, `guessed_user_id`, `created_at`, `correct` (bool).

Points/standings are computed by aggregating `guesses.correct` grouped by guesser **within one `game_id`** — this naturally gives "reset every game" for free (a new game has a fresh empty set of guesses) without needing an explicit reset action, and naturally gives "wiped when the lobby closes" for free too (cascade-deleting a lobby's `games` removes its `game_rounds` and `guesses`), while `posts` survive automatically since they no longer reference the lobby at all.

Admin is *derived*: Admin = `profiles.is_admin`. Organizer is **not** purely derived — it's the `lobbies.organizer_id` field, updated by the transfer rule above (it starts out equal to `created_by`, then can diverge).
